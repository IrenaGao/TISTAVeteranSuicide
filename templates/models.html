MODEL EXPLANATION

Logistic Regression Model

We used logistic regression to predict the percentage risk of suicide based on a veteran's answers. Our model has an accuracy of 65% and recall of 53%, which means that our model is likely to correctly identify a veteran's risk, along with which veterans are suicidal. The final features we used were “CurrIPSub”, “CurrResSub”, “hc24”, “psy24”, “vision24”, “CumDaysUsein24MoPrior” and “mhdisprior24mo” with the interaction term between “CurrIPSub” and “CumDaysUsein24MoPrior.” Through statistical methods such as wrapper-based feature selection and cross validation, we determined that these variables are the most effective predictors of suicide risk.

NLP Twitter Model

We used NLP text classification to analyze text from Twitter tweets and predict suicide risk from a psychological perspective. Inspired by the success of similar models used to predict depression risk in Twitter users, we trained a recurrent neural network based (LSTM) model using sentiment analysis to produce a depression score for each individual tweet. Using the Twint API, we scraped from Twitter and collected over 20000 suicide-related tweets, which contain suicidal keywords like “tired of living”, “better off dead”, “my suicide note”, “my suicide letter”, “end my life” and “depression” etc. We further used Vader and Textblob sentiment polarity scores as the proxy labels for our tweet data set. To improve the accuracy of these proxy labels, we used thresholds to decide whether a certain tweet is considered suicidal or non-suicidal. For example, when threshold is set to 0.2, tweets with polarity lower than 0.2 will be labelled as 0 as in suicidal and 1 for non-suicidal. Finally, we trained the LSTM model on our labeled data, and as a result we got an 89% validation F1 score. This indicates that our model has a high precision and recall, and that we can indeed model depression/suicide risk as a function of the content of Twitter posts.
